The deal didn't die. You just stopped following up.

By InkLock Team · August 23, 2026 · 5 min read
The short version

Most proposals don't get a no. They get silence. The follow-up is where deals are actually won or lost, and 'I followed up once' isn't a system. Build a real cadence and let it run.

Silence isn't a no

You sent the quote. Then nothing. So you assume they passed and you let it go. Most of the time, they didn't pass at all. They got busy. It slid down the list. Life happened on their end and your proposal got buried under forty other things. A sent proposal with no follow-up isn't a lost deal. It's a deal you talked yourself out of.

"I followed up once" is not a follow-up system

One nudge two days later, then you go quiet because a second message feels pushy. Meanwhile the person who actually needed five touches and a reminder the following week just hired someone who kept showing up. Persistence doesn't read as annoying when it's genuinely helpful. It reads as "this person actually wants my business."

The follow-up cadence that closes

  • Day 1. Proposal lands with a clear, specific next step. Not "let me know your thoughts," but "want to hop on a 15-minute call Thursday to walk through it?"
  • Day 2 or 3. A quick "any questions on this?" check-in. Low pressure, easy to reply to.
  • Day 7. A nudge with an actual reason to move, like a relevant note or a deadline, not a hollow "just checking in."
  • Day 14. The honest "should I close this out on my end?" It works shockingly well, because it gives a busy person an easy moment to say "no wait, I'm still in."
  • Then stop, and nurture. Drop them into a long-term sequence. Not every yes is a yes-right-now, and that's fine.
The deal you think died is usually just one follow-up you never sent.

This is exactly where systems carry the marketing

You don't follow up consistently because you're busy doing the actual work you got hired for. So the follow-up can't depend on you remembering, because you won't. Automate the cadence: the reminders fire, the emails queue, the next touch is teed up whether or not you thought about it. Keep the real conversation human, let the system make sure no proposal ever slips through the cracks again. That's revenue you already earned, sitting one message away from the bank.

Stop letting good deals die of silence. Build the follow-up once, let it run, and watch how many "no responses" quietly turn into signatures. Simple as that.

Frequently asked questions

Why do proposals go unanswered?

Usually not because the answer is no. People get busy, the proposal slides down their list, and without a follow-up nudge it simply gets forgotten. Silence is far more often a pause than a rejection.

How many times should I follow up on a proposal?

More than once. A workable cadence is roughly day one, day two or three, day seven, and day fourteen, then a move into long-term nurture. Many deals close on the third or fourth touch, not the first.

Isn't following up multiple times annoying?

Not when each touch is helpful and low-pressure. Persistence that adds value or makes saying yes easy reads as genuine interest, not nagging. The trick is giving a reason to respond, not just 'checking in.'

How do I follow up consistently when I'm busy?

Automate the cadence. Let reminders fire and emails queue automatically so the follow-up happens without you remembering, while you keep the actual conversation personal. The system guarantees no proposal falls through the cracks.
